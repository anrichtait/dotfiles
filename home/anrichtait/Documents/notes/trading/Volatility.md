## Definition: 
Volatility refers to how much the price of a financial asset changes over a period of time.It is used to measure how much risk or uncertainty is associated with an asset.

## High Volatility:
- **Characteristics:** The price of an asset changes rapidly and by large amounts in a short period of time.
- **Impact:** Indicates higher risk, as the price of an asset can swing significantly up or down, making it less predictable to trade with.
- **Example:** During times of economic uncertainty stock markets may experience high volatility in response to news and events.
## Low Volatility:
- **Characteristics:** The price of an asset changes slowly and by small amounts, indicating more stability.
- **Impact:** Low volatility is associated with lower risk, making it more stable and predictable to trade with.
- **Example:** Blue-chip stocks, ([[Key Words and Terms]]) often exhibit low volatility compared to smaller, more speculative companies.
## Measurement:
- **VIX (Volatility Index):** Indicates how much the price of a financial asset fluctuates over time. High volatility suggests greater price swings and higher risk, while low volatility indicates more stable prices and lower risk. Understanding volatility is crucial for assessing the risk and potential reward associated with different investments.
- **Standard Deviation:** 
	- **Definition:** Standard deviation is a statistical measure that shows how much a set of numbers (in this case, asset prices) varies from the average (mean) of those numbers.
	- **Application to Volatility:** In the context of volatility, standard deviation measures how much the price of an asset (like a stock) fluctuates around its average price over a certain period.
- **How It Works:**
	1. **Calculate the Average (Mean) Price:**
		Calculate the average price of the asset over specific period of time. For example, if you look at the daily closing prices of a stock over 10 days, you add up all the 10 prices and divide by 10 to get the average price.
	2. **Find the Deviations:** 
	 Next you determine how much each day's price differs from the average price. This difference is called the *deviation*.
	3. **Square the Deviations:**
		To avoid negative numbers cancelling out positive numbers, you square each deviation (multiply it by itself), this gives you the squared deviations.
	4. **Calculate the Average Squared Deviations:**
		Add up all the squared deviations and then divide by the number of prices to get the *variance*.
	5. **Take the Square Root:**
		Take the square root of the variance. This will give you the standard deviation, which is the measure of how much the prices are spread out around the average.
- **What It Tells You:**
	- **High Standard Deviation**:
		If the standard deviation is high it means the asset's prices are spread out over a wide range, indicating high volatility. Meaning it's more unpredictable and risky.
	- **Low Standard Deviation:**
		If the standard deviation is low it means the asset's prices are close to the average, indicating low volatility. The asset's prices are more stable, making it more predictable and less risky.
- **Example**:
	 - Suppose a stock has daily closing prices over 5 days of $100, $105, $95, $110, and $90.
		- The average price over these 5 days is $100.
		- The deviations from the average are: $0, $5, -$5, $10, -$10.
		- Squaring these deviations gives: $0, $25, $25, $100, $100.
		- The variance (average of squared deviations) is $50.
		- The standard deviation (square root of $50) is approximately $7.07.
	- **Interpretation**:
		- The standard deviation of $7.07 means the stock's price typically deviates by about $7.07 from the average price of $100, indicating the level of volatility.
