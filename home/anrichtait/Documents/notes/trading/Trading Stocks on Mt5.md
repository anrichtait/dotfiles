Stocks can be traded through Forex brokers. These platforms trade in Contract for Difference (CFD). CFDs are like shares but the buyer does not own the share. Instead they "speculate" on whether the share price will increase (buy order) or decrease (sell order). So the trader aims to benefit from the difference in price from the point of the trade to the closing of the trade.

# Differences between an EXCHANGE and CFDs:

- CFDs are traded in two directions, long and short.
- CFDs are always traded on margin, unlike traditional accounts where that is an option.

# Factors that influence stock prices:

## Dividends: 
- __What are Dividends?__
  A portion of the company's earning that are distributed to shareholders, either as cash or more shares. This is a reward/incentive to hold the company's stock.
- __Ex-Dividend Date__:
  The date on or after which new buyers of the stock will not receive the declared dividend. 
- __Price Adjustments on the Ex-Div Date__:
	1. __Price Adjustment__: The stock price usually drops by the price of the dividend, because the amount is no longer part of the company's stock price as new buyers will not receive it.
	2. __Market Behaviour__: For example, if a company declares a dividend of $1 per share and the stock was trading at `$50` on the ex-dividend date you might see the stock price drop to `$49`. This reflects that the companies cash reserves were depleted by the amount given out.
	3. __Theoretical Drop__: This drop is not always exact and there are many other factors that affect the overall price of a stock. However due to how a company's cash is depleted this should reflect in the stock price.
## Market Sentiment:
- __Market Sentiment__: The overall attitude investors have towards the financial markets. It reflects the general direction the markets are moving driven by factors such as economic indicators, news, global events, and investor psychology. When sentiment is __positive__ investors are generally confident leading to a rise in stock prices. Conversely, when sentiment is __negative__ stock prices fall in price.
- __Impact on Stock Prices__: 
	- If the market sentiment is __negative__ (in decline/bearish) most stocks tend to fall in price because investors are selling off their holdings or are less willing to buy.
	- If the market sentiment is __positive__ (bullish), most stocks tend to rise in price because investors are buying, expecting prices to go up.
- __Using an Index as an Indicator__: 
	- An __index__ is a statistical measure of a portion of the stock market. It is made up of a selected group of stocks which can be an indicator of the performance of other stocks on the market.
	- The __ASX - All Ordinaries__ is an example of such an index, it represents the 500 largest companies listed on the Australian Securities Exchange (ASX). Another example is the __NASDAQ-100__ which monitors the largest 100 companies in the U.S (especially in the tech sector). These are good way to see the overall market sentiment for the market in a region.
	- Traders can use the indices to get an idea of the overall market sentiment. If the index is trending upwards then it suggests the overall market is doing well, therefore most stock in the market will likely rise in price.
	- __Global Influence of Indices__: While an index reflects the performance of a specific region or sector in a region (NASDAQ = mainly tech), global events can trigger dips or rises in other markets. For example a major economic event in the U.S could impact the NASDAQ and ASX as well.

## Company Earnings:
- **Company Earnings:** Refer to the financial results a company reports including:
	- Revenue
	- Profit
	- Earnings per share
- **Market Expectations:**
	- **Pre-Earnings Expectations:** Analysts and investors form expectations about a company based on a few factors that set the benchmark for how the market anticipates the company will perform:
		- Historical performance
		- Industry/Sector trends
		- Company guidance
	- **How This Impacts the Stock Price:**
		- **Earnings Below Expectations:**
			- **Outcome:** If the earnings are lower than expected the stock price typically falls.
			- **Reason**: Lower than expected earnings can signal potential problems or under performance, which leads investors to sell shares.
		- **Earnings Above Expectations:**
			- **Outcome**: If the earnings are above expectation then the company's stock usually rises in price.
			- **Reason:** Higher earnings indicate a strong financial performance or shows more growth potential, which attracts new investors.

## Unforeseen Circumstances:
- **Unforeseen Circumstances:** Refers to events that are unexpected or beyond control, examples include:
	- Natural disasters
	- Political instability
	- Pandemics
- **Impact on Stock Prices:**
	- **General Market Impact:** Unforeseen events often cause market uncertainty, leading to [[Volatility]], and potentially sharp declines in stock prices as investors react to the news.
	- **Sector-Specific Impact:** Certain industries are more heavily impacted than others depending on the circumstance. For example, natural disasters can heavily impact insurance companies, construction firms, and supply chain related businesses.
- **Impact on Insurance Companies:**
	- **Effect of Natural Disasters:** Insurance companies are particularly vulnerable to natural disasters as they may face large payouts for claims (essentially losing value). Leading to a decrease in their profitability and a drop in their stock prices.

## Mergers, Takeovers, and Acquisitions:
- **[[Key Words and Terms|Mergers, Takeovers and Acquisitions: ]]** These are corporate actions where two companies combine (merger), one company acquires another (takeover), or a company buys another company (acquisition). These events can significantly impact the stock prices of the companies involved.
- **Impact on Stock Prices:** 
	- **Company being acquired (Target Company):**
		- **Price Increase:** When a company is taken over or acquired it's stock prices typically requires.
		- **Reason:** The acquiring company usually offers a price higher than the current market price to persuade shareholders to sell their shares. This premium reflects the expected benefits of the acquisition, such as increased market share or new assets.
		- **Example:** If Company A is acquiring Company B, Company B's stock price might jump as investors anticipate the takeover offer and it's benefits.
	- **Acquiring Company:** 
		- **Price Decrease:** The stock price of the company that is taking over or acquiring another company might decline.
		- **Reason:** Investors might be concerned about the costs associated with the acquisition, such as the debt taken on or the risk of integrating the new business. This can lead to a temporary drop in the acquirer's stock price.
		- **Example:** If Company A is buying Company B, Company A's stock price might drop as investors weigh the costs and potential risks of the acquisition.
	- **Short-Term Imbalance:** 
		- The immediate effect of a merger usually causes an imbalance, with the target company's stock price rising and the acquiring company's stock price falling.
	- **Potential Long-Term Balance:** 
		- Over time, the stock prices can balance out or even result in a net gain if the merger is successful and creates value for shareholders. However, this depends on the effectiveness of the merger and the market's perception of its success.

## Interest Rate Changes:
- **Interest Rate Changes:** Refers to adjustments made by the central banks to the benchmark interest rates. This influences the cost of borrowing and the return on savings across the economy.
- **Impact on Investors and Stock Prices:**
	- **Market Reaction:**
		- When the interest rates are changed it directly impacts the broader economy. Including home loan rates, consumer spending and business investment.
		- A **rate increase** generally leads to higher borrowing costs which can reduce consumer spending and slow down economic growth, often causing stock prices to fall.
		- A **rate decrease** usually lowers borrowing costs, which can boost consumer spending and business investment, leading to higher stock prices.
	- **Investor Behaviour:** 
		- **Higher Interest Rates:** Investors might shift their focus from stocks to fixed-income investments like bonds, which become more attractive due to higher yields. Potentially leading to a decline in stock prices.
		- **Lower Interest Rates:** Stocks may become more attractive compared to low-yield bonds, encouraging investment in the stock market, which can drive up stock prices.
	- **Impact on Specific Sectors:**
		- Sectors like real estate and financials can be more sensitive to rate changes. For example, higher rates can lead to higher mortgage costs, impacting real estate companies, while financial institutions might benefit from higher lending rates.
- **Example of Interest Rate Changes Impacting the Market (2024)**	
	In **August 2024**, the Federal Reserve (Fed) held interest rates steady, signaling potential rate cuts in 2024 due to evolving economic conditions. Despite a slight uptick in inflation projections, the Fed's stance reflects its expectation of a stronger economy in the coming years. 
	
	The Fed's decision to maintain rates at the current level and project rate cuts for 2024 positively influenced market sentiment. The **S&P 500** and other equity indices have been forecasted to achieve significant growth, largely due to anticipated rate cuts, declining inflation, and rising corporate earnings. This expectation of lower future rates has caused a positive reaction in the market, with stock prices likely to rise as borrowing costs decrease and investment becomes more attractive.
	
	This event underscores the influence of central bank decisions on stock market performance, especially in how investors react to forecasts of future economic conditions and rate changes. *(See source 1 and 2)*

## Evolution of the Industry Sector:
- **Industry Evolution:** Changes and developments within a specific industry sector that can significantly influence the stock prices of companies operating within that sector.
- **Impact on Stock Prices:**
	- **Trend Following:** The majority of companies in an industry sector tend to follow prevailing trends. For example if a sector like technology is experiencing rapid growth due to innovation or increased demand, stock prices of companies in that sector are likely to rise.
	- **Exceptions to the Trend:** Not all companies will follow the sector trend. Other factors such as company-specific performance, management decisions or external economic conditions can cause some companies to deviate from the general trend in the sector.
	- **Sector-Specific Risks:** Factors like regulatory changes, technological advancements or shifts in consumer preferences can also impact the sector and consequently, the stock prices of companies within that sector.
-  **Examples of Companies Following and Not Following Industry Trends:**
	- **Example of "Trend Following":**
		- **AI Boom Post-ChatGPT**: When OpenAI released ChatGPT, it sparked a massive surge in interest around artificial intelligence. This led many other companies in the AI sector to follow the trend by developing or integrating AI capabilities into their products and services. Companies like **Google** and **Microsoft** quickly adapted by enhancing their own AI-driven features, leading to significant stock price increases and overall success in the market. Those companies that invested in AI technologies saw substantial gains, aligning with the broader industry trend of increasing AI adoption.
	- **Example of "Exceptions to the Trend":**
		- **Digital Shift in Gaming**: The gaming industry has increasingly shifted towards digital platforms, especially during and after the COVID-19 pandemic. Companies like **Steam** thrived in this environment, benefiting from their strong digital presence and ability to serve a global audience with minimal physical infrastructure. However, **GameStop**, which was heavily reliant on in-store sales, struggled to keep up with this digital transformation. While GameStop did see a temporary stock price boost during the short squeeze event and a leadership change involving a prominent online market entrepreneur, it largely missed out on the sustained growth experienced by more digitally focused companies in the sector. This is a prime example of a company not following the prevailing industry trend and facing challenges as a result.

## Stock Buybacks
- **Stock Buybacks:** Occurs when a company purchases its own shares from the market, reducing the total number of shares available to the public.
- **Impact on Stock Prices:**
	- **Reduction in Supply:** By buying back its own shares the company decreases the number of shares available in the market which can increase the stock's price due to the reduced supply.
	- **Signal of Confidence:** A stock buyback often signals to investors that the company believes its stock is undervalued which can boost investor confidence and lead to a rise in the stock price.
	- **Increased Earnings Per Share (EPS):** With fewer shares outstanding the company's earnings are spread over a smaller number of shares, often resulting in higher EPS which can further drive up the stock price.
- **Reasons for Stock Buybacks:**
	- **Excess Cash:** Companies with excess cash may use buybacks as a way to return value to shareholders, especially if they have no immediate investment opportunities that offer a higher return.
	- **Capital Structure Management:** Buybacks can help adjust the company's capital structure by reducing the number of equity shares and potentially increasing the proportion of debt in the company's financing.
	- **Offset Dilution:** Companies may repurchase shares to offset dilution caused by stock options and convertible securities issued to employees or executives.
	- **Improve Financial Metrics:** Buybacks can improve key financial metrics such as EPS and return on equity (ROE), which can be attractive to investors and analysts.
	- **Flexibility:** Unlike dividends, which create a recurring obligation, buybacks are discretionary and can be adjusted based on the company's financial situation and market conditions.
